How to use Fundamentals

A quick guide to screening companies, reading a company page, understanding the intrinsic-value estimates, and keeping track of what you're watching.

Screening

The Screener is the way in. It lists the whole universe of ~3,000 US companies; the filters narrow it:

  • Search — match a ticker or company name.
  • Sector and Index — restrict to a sector, or to S&P 500 / Russell 3000 members.
  • Metric filter — pick any metric and an optional min/max on its latest reported fiscal-year value. Results are ranked by that metric, high to low.

Every filter lives in the URL, so a screen is a link you can bookmark or share.

Reading a company

A company page shows the reported statements — income, balance sheet, and cash flow — alongside metrics derived uniformly across every company:

Profitability & margins
Gross, operating, and net margins; return measures.
Leverage & liquidity
Debt-to-equity and related balance-sheet ratios.
Cash generation
Free cash flow and its components.
Valuation multiples
P/E and peers, priced at the company's real fiscal close (so non-December filers aren't mispriced against a calendar year-end).

Quarterly figures are reconciled back to the annual filing, so a fiscal-year total always ties out to what the company actually reported.

Intrinsic value & margin of safety

Each company is valued four independent ways, so you can compare methods rather than trust one number:

  • Graham — a defensive earnings-and-growth formula.
  • Discounted cash flow (DCF) — future cash flows discounted to today.
  • Owner Earnings — Buffett-style cash earnings.
  • EPS-CAGR — forward earnings projected from the trailing growth rate.

Each is estimated under bull, mid, and bear assumptions. The margin of safety is the gap between an estimate and the current price: positive means the estimate sits above the market (a discount), negative means the market is already paying more than the estimate.

These are model outputs, not price targets — treat them as a starting point for your own judgment.

Watchlists, favorites & history

With an account you can keep track of what you're researching:

Watchlists
Group companies into named lists; add or remove a ticker from its company page. Everyone gets a default list to start.
Favorites
A one-click star for companies you want at hand.
History
The companies you've viewed recently, most recent first — a trail back to where you were.

Accounts

Screening and company pages are open to everyone. Watchlists, favorites, and history need a free account. Sign up or log in — it takes an email and a password.

Data & disclaimer

Figures are derived from SEC EDGAR filings and market prices and refresh each time the data pipeline runs, so they lag the very latest filing. For programmatic access, the same read model is available as a JSON API.

This is an informational research tool, not investment advice, and may contain errors — verify anything you'd act on against primary sources.
